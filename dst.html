A Delaware Statutory Trust

A Delaware Statutory Trust (DST) is a separate legal entity formed as a trust under Delaware law. If properly structured, the DST will be classified as a grantor trust for federal income tax purposes and, as a result, the purchaser of a beneficial interest in the trust will acquire an undivided interest in the asset(s) held by the DST.

Property held legally as a DST qualify as 1031 property under IRS Revenue Ruling 2004-86. That ruling dictated that DSTs would have to meet certain parameters designed to protect hard-earned investor capital. These parameters are known as the "7 deadly sins" of DST investing.

In application, investors own their beneficial interest in the assets(s) by buying their pro-rata share in the trust. For example, if a DST offering was raising $100mill and an investor purchased a $1mill equity interest in the Trust, they’d own a 1.0% beneficial interest in both the trust & underlying real estate asset(s). This would include 1.0% of all revenue, expenses, debt payments, and potential future appreciation.

The Sponsor of a DST offering buys the asset(s) and then raises equity from private, accredited investors. DST offerings vary greatly by asset class, US locale, and business plan.

The managing Trustee of the DST is either the Sponsor or an affiliate of the Sponsor. The DST holds legal title to 100% of the interest in the property.

Tax reporting for a DST is done on a Schedule E utilizing property operating information provided by the Sponsor.
